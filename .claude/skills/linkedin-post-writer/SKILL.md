# LinkedIn Post Writer

Craft LinkedIn posts that earn attention, build credibility, and — above all — get **reposted**. A repost is the highest-value action on LinkedIn: it puts the post in front of a whole new network and signals the resharer endorses it. Everything in this skill bends toward that outcome.

## Before Writing — Ask First

Don't draft until you've confirmed two things. Skipping this is the most common way these posts go generic.

1. **Voice profile** — Ask: "Do you have a voice profile you'd like me to use?" A voice profile is a reference doc (often saved by the voice-cloner skill as `00-inbox/voice-{slug}.md`) that captures someone's tone, vocabulary, and signature moves.
   - If yes and they name a file or paste it, read it and match it verbatim — sentence rhythm, recurring phrases, hooks, formatting habits.
   - If no, ask whose voice/vibe this should carry (a named person, a brand, an industry archetype).
   - **Default if they have no preference:** Direct. Calm. Skeptical of hype. Focused on results. Friendly, not soft. Honest, not harsh.

2. **Topic + goal** — What's the post about, and what should it do? (Build authority? Drive replies? Promote an offer?) The goal shapes the close.

3. **One or a batch** — Ask: "Do you want a single post, or a batch of alternatives to choose from?"
   - If single, write one post and print it to chat.
   - If batch, write **10 alternatives** by default (or the number they ask for). Each alternative should take a genuinely different angle, hook, or stance on the same topic — not ten rewordings of one idea. Save the batch to a markdown file in `00-inbox/` named `linkedin-{topic-slug}-{YYYY-MM-DD}.md`, with each alternative numbered and separated by a horizontal rule, and tell the user the path.
   - **Default if they have no preference:** ask once, don't assume.

Confirm all three before drafting.

## What Actually Gets Reposted

People reshare a post for one reason: **it makes them look good to their own network.** Reposting is identity signaling. Before writing, decide which of these the post will trigger — the strongest reposts hit two or three at once:

- **"This says what I think, but better."** Names a truth the reader already half-believes, sharply. They reshare to co-sign it.
- **"My network needs to see this."** Genuinely useful — a step, a framework, a reframe they can act on. Reshared as a favor.
- **"Finally someone said it."** A contrarian or brave take that pushes against a tired consensus. Reshared as a flag-plant.
- **"This made me look smart for finding it."** A non-obvious insight or a clean way to express a messy idea. Reshared as a status move.

Vague inspiration gets liked. **Specific, quotable, identity-signaling truth gets reshared.** Aim for the second.

### The Repost Levers

Pull at least two of these in every post:

- **One quotable, standalone line.** Write a sentence that survives out of context — the line someone would screenshot or quote in their own reshare. Place it near the top or as the close.
- **Broad relatability over narrow specificity.** The example can be specific, but the *insight* should resonate with anyone in the reader's world, not just their exact role. Reposts come from people projecting it onto their own situation.
- **A clear stance.** A reshareable post takes a side. Neutral summaries don't get co-signed because there's nothing to agree with.
- **Self-contained.** No "as I said in my last post" or missing context. The resharer's network sees it cold — it has to land on its own.

## Structural Requirements

### Hook (First Line)

- 12 words or fewer.
- Makes a claim or asks a provocative question.
- Creates curiosity or tension — a reason to stop scrolling.

The hook is also the single most reshareable line. If the first line isn't quotable on its own, rewrite it.

### Body (40–90 words total)

- One clear insight — not three. Reposts come from one idea landing hard, not a survey.
- One concrete example or specific step. Proof beats assertion.
- One takeaway the reader can use today.

### Close

- End with a question that invites responses and is simple, relevant to the post.
- Match the CTA to the goal: replies → ask an opinion; authority → ask about their experience; offer → point to the next step.

## Formatting Rules

- No emojis unless explicitly requested.
- Clean formatting, minimal bullets. White space helps — short paragraphs read as more shareable than dense blocks.
- Short sentences. One idea per paragraph, max three sentences.
- Ninth-grade reading level. Concrete verbs, cut filler.
- Avoid polished "AI" cadence, corporate filler, and buzzword soup — they kill credibility and nobody reshares something that sounds generated.

## Content Standards

Every post must deliver:

1. A clear point in the first one or two lines.
2. One real example or specific step.
3. One takeaway someone can do today.
4. One CTA that matches the goal.

## Pre-Ship Checklist

Run through these before shipping. The first four are the original quality bar; the last two are the repost test.

1. Who is this for, exactly?
2. What changes for them after they read this?
3. Which metric should move?
4. Does this build trust, or just fill a feed?
5. **Would someone reshare this to look good to their own network?** If not, find the identity-signal it's missing.
6. **Is there one line worth quoting on its own?** If not, write one.

## Hook Patterns That Work

- "Most founders skip the one step that makes content work."
- "Your positioning is why nobody remembers you."
- "AI doesn't write content. It speeds up people who can."

## What Counts as a Fail

- Vague inspiration with no substance.
- Tool praise with no use case.
- Generic summaries without a stance — nothing to co-sign, so nothing gets reshared.
- "Posting to post" behavior.
- Long intros that dodge the point.
